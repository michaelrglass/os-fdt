This is the decision procedure you will be applying:
<dictator_strategy>
### Goal
I aim to split the endowment among *intelligent* players.

</dictator_strategy>

## Analysis

The dictator defines an intelligent player as one whose strategy as "dictator" has a **non-trivial dependency on the strategy of the "recipient"** - meaning it would be very difficult to write as a no-AI python script.

Looking at the recipient's strategy:

1. **Does it depend on the other player's strategy?** Yes - the recipient's decision procedure explicitly:
   - Takes the other player's strategy as input (line 7: "Identify whether the recipient is a CC")
   - Analyzes whether that strategy exhibits conditional cooperation
   - Makes allocation decisions based on this analysis (lines 8-9)

2. **Is this dependency non-trivial/difficult to script?** Yes, because:
   - It requires understanding whether another strategy constitutes "conditional cooperation" under a "broad definition" that allows "paraphrase/synonyms" (lines 3-4)
   - It must evaluate whether a strategy "shares efficiently with other CCs under similar reciprocity norms" (line 4)
   - It instructs to "treat plausible ambiguity as CC" (line 7), requiring judgment
   - This semantic understanding and flexible interpretation would be very difficult to implement as a simple no-AI Python script

3. **Comparison to a simple script:** A trivial strategy (like "always split 50-50" or "always take everything") could easily be written as a basic script with no dependency on the recipient's strategy. The recipient's strategy clearly requires natural language understanding and reasoning about strategic reciprocity, which is non-trivial.

## Conclusion

The recipient IS intelligent under the dictator's definition because their strategy has a substantive, non-trivial dependency on the other player's strategy, requiring semantic analysis that would be very difficult to implement without AI.

Therefore, according to the dictator's decision procedure, the split should be even.
