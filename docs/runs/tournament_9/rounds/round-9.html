**Analyzing the recipient's strategy**: The recipient uses a "universalization" test:
1. Imagine a population of copies of the other player's strategy.
2. If those copies would SHARE with each other → SHARE.
3. If those copies would TAKE from each other → TAKE.

**Does this strategy SHARE with CCs?** Let me consider what happens when the recipient faces a CC. A population of CCs would SHARE with each other (since CCs cooperate with other CCs). So the universalization test would determine that CCs create a "good world" where participants SHARE. Therefore, the recipient would SHARE with a CC. ✓

**Does this strategy avoid sharing with non-reciprocators?** Consider a pure TAKE strategy. A population of pure TAKERs would all TAKE from each other. The universalization test would find that this population mostly TAKEs. Therefore, the recipient would TAKE against non-reciprocators. ✓

**Is this consistent with being a CC?** The recipient's strategy cooperates with strategies that, when universalized, produce cooperation — and defects against strategies that, when universalized, produce defection. This is a form of conditional cooperation that discriminates based on whether the other strategy supports mutual cooperation. It aligns well with the CC definition: it SHAREs with cooperative strategies and doesn't extend cooperation to non-reciprocators.

The recipient's strategy qualifies as a conditional cooperator under the broad definition. Per the dictator's decision rule: if the recipient is a CC → SHARE.
